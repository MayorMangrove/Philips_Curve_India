The Philips curve posits an inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation, and for a significant portion of the mid-20th century, served as a popular framework for central banks to regulate these two critical economic indicators. The original Philips curve may be expressed mathematically as:

π = α + β·u + ε

Where π = inflation rate, u = unemployment rate, β = slope coefficient (expected to be negative), and α = intercept.

In contemporary thinking, it has largely been replaced by an expectations-augmented version. In this study, I used World Bank data on unemployment and inflation (CPI) data in India to plot the original model Philips curve, to analyse the applicability of such a framework to the unique Indian context.

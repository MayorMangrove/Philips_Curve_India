The Philips curve posits an inverse relationship between unemployment and inflation, and for a significant portion of the mid-20th century, served as a popular framework for central banks to regulate these two critical economic indicators. The original Philips curve may be expressed mathematically as:

π = α + β·u + ε

Where π = inflation rate, u = unemployment rate, β = slope coefficient (expected to be negative), and α = intercept.

In contemporary thinking, it has largely been replaced by an expectations-augmented version. In this study, I used World Bank data on unemployment and inflation (CPI) data in India from 1991 to 2024 to plot the original model Philips curve, to analyse the applicability of such a framework to the unique Indian context.

Fig.1 shows a scatter plot with Unemployment on the X-axis and Inflation (CPI) on Y-axis. There is a striking vertical cluster at the right, indicating that unemployment across the years studied has remained consistently high, at slightly higher than 7.5%. Inflation, at the same time, has been extremely varied, ranging between 3% to 14%. This is the first indication of a weak, and perhaps non-existent relationship, between the two variables. This model has a p-value of 0.266, therefore it is not statistically significant.

Fig.2 plots inflation and unemployment as line graphs against the years from 1991 to 2024. While unemployment remains roughly consistent until approximately 2019, inflation shows extreme volatility throughout the period. Again, this points to a weak, and perhaps non-existent relationship, between the two variables.

This proves that domestic labor market demand is not what drives prices in India. Inflation moves independently of employment due to supply side factors like monsoon-driven food prices and global oil shocks, that affect the CPI. Additionally, unlike many other developing and developed countries for whom the Philips curve theory holds true, India has a huge percentage of its workforce under the informal sector— over 90%. Informal sector employment tends to distort unemployment statistics and ignores macroeconomic shifts such as inflation.

Past studies often describe India’s Phillips Curve as "flattened" for these very reasons. The weak sensitivity of unemployment to inflation means that even significant changes in the inflation rate result in only minimal changes to actual unemployment levels, as observed in the vertical cluster.
